Amsterdam · WorldPride 2026

One Million by the Canals: The World Comes to Amsterdam

From July 25 to August 2, the Dutch capital becomes the stage for the planet's most ambitious celebration of queer life. The Canal Parade on August 1 is unlike anything else on Earth.

Amsterdam was always an unlikely candidate for the world's most spectacular Pride. A city of 900,000 residents, it would host a million visitors in a single week — and route them through waterways built in the seventeenth century. Yet WorldPride 2026 chose it for precisely that reason: the Canal Parade on August 1, with decorated barges moving through the city's concentric ring of canals, offers a visual and logistical intimacy that no boulevard march can replicate.

The event — running from July 25 through August 2 — draws delegations from 44 countries. Travel platforms reported booking surges of over 200% across several European cities. Hotels from Brussels to Hamburg fielded overflow bookings from Amsterdam-bound travellers.

WorldPride is not merely a celebration — it is an event that rotates to cities where its visibility carries symbolic weight. Amsterdam, with its long history of progressive legislation on same-sex partnerships, accepted the designation as both an honour and a responsibility.

"There is no pride for some of us without liberation for all of us."

— Marsha P. Johnson · Stonewall, 1969 · Invoked by NYC Pride 2026
New York City · June 28

NYC Pride Declares: "For All of Us"

Heritage of Pride's theme for the 2026 New York City March is not chosen lightly. In a year of federal rollbacks on trans healthcare access and DEI programmes, the organisation invoked the words of Marsha P. Johnson to name what Pride is for: not celebration for some, but liberation for everyone.

The June 28 march — timed to the 57th anniversary of the Stonewall uprising — saw 75,000 registered marchers across 695 groups, with an estimated 2.5 million spectators lining the route from Midtown to the West Village.

São Paulo · June 2026

São Paulo: 3 to 4 Million on Avenida Paulista

By raw numbers, São Paulo Pride is the largest single gathering of LGBTQ+ people on the planet. The June 2026 march along Avenida Paulista drew estimates of three to four million participants — a figure that puts it in the company of the world's largest religious gatherings and national celebrations.

The march carries particular weight in Brazil, where LGBTQ+ communities have navigated years of political hostility. Avenida Paulista, São Paulo's great commercial artery, becomes for one afternoon a space where queer Brazilians are not a minority — they are the city. São Paulo Pride began in 1997 with two thousand attendees. In less than three decades it has grown by a factor of two thousand.

Delhi · February 8, 2026

Delhi Pride Returns — and Breaks Its Own Record

After missing 2025 entirely, the Delhi Queer Pride Parade came back on February 8, 2026 with what organisers describe as the highest turnout in the event's eighteen-year history.

The parade began at Connaught Place and marched to Jantar Mantar — the same corridor where protestors have gathered for every cause the Republic has fought over. The symbolism is not accidental. Delhi Pride has always understood itself as political as much as celebratory.

The march was first held in 2008, one year before the Delhi High Court read down Section 377 in 2009. At the time, a few hundred gathered while criminalisation remained in force. In 2026, with decriminalisation eight years in the past, the march has grown into something the founders could not have imagined.

Parliament · Historic First

Menaka Guruswamy Enters Parliament — India's First Openly LGBTQ+ MP

The lawyer who co-argued the Section 377 case before the Supreme Court is now a legislator. Menaka Guruswamy, elected to the Rajya Sabha on a Trinamool Congress ticket in 2026, became the first openly LGBTQ+ member of India's Parliament — a milestone the community's long legal fight made possible, and that she now carries into the chamber itself.

"Visibility is not a reward for rights won — it is the condition under which rights become real."

— PRISM editorial board · Issue 3
Parliament · Policy

The Amendment That Alarmed Advocates

The same Parliament that now includes India's first openly LGBTQ+ member also passed the Transgender Persons (Protection of Rights) Amendment Bill, 2026 — legislation that rights groups say moves in the opposite direction from the name it carries.

The bill removes a transgender person's right to self-identify their gender, replacing it with a requirement for examination by a medical board. Advocates who spent years winning those protections call it a quiet erasure.

What's in a Flag? A Guide to Pride's Many Banners

The six-stripe rainbow flag, designed by Gilbert Baker in 1978, remains the universal emblem of queer visibility. But the rainbow alone cannot carry the weight of every identity it is asked to represent. Over the past four decades, the community has responded with more than twenty distinct flags — each a declaration that a particular identity deserves its own symbol, its own colours, its own history.

Rainbow
Gilbert Baker, 1978. Universal symbol of queer pride and visibility.
Trans Pride
Monica Helms, 1999. Light blue, pink, and white for trans visibility worldwide.
Bisexual
Michael Page, 1998. Pink, lavender, and royal blue for attraction across genders.
Non-binary
Kye Rowan, 2014. Yellow, white, purple, and black for identities outside the binary.
Asexual
AVEN, 2010. Black, grey, white, and purple for the spectrum of asexual identities.
Intersex
OII Australia, 2013. Yellow and purple — colours historically unused by other movements.
Language

The Language Keeps Evolving — and That's the Point

Language is not merely the way we describe identities — it is the way identities become visible. The expansion of the acronym from LGBT to LGBTQ+ to LGBTQIA+ is not an act of bureaucratic padding. Each addition is an acknowledgment that a community of people existed before they had a shared name for themselves.

The word "queer" was a slur within living memory. Its reclamation — now so complete that it has become the preferred umbrella term for many — is one of the more remarkable reversals in modern language. The word was taken back by the people it was used against, stripped of its weaponised meaning, and re-inflected as an act of self-naming.

The "+" in LGBTQ+ is a placeholder — an acknowledgment that the acronym will always be shorter than the reality it tries to name. Language chases identity, not the other way around.

"Getting the language right is not political correctness. It is the minimum courtesy of learning someone's name."

— PRISM editorial note
A short glossary
Queer — Reclaimed umbrella term for LGBTQ+ people and experiences.
Gender-expansive — Identities that stretch beyond the male/female binary.
Intersex — Natural variation in sex characteristics. Not the same as gender identity.
Asexual — Little or no sexual attraction. Distinct from aromantic.
Cisgender — When one's gender identity matches the sex assigned at birth.
